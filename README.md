# Benyuan: Rewriting the Foundation of the Economy

*English Edition — a re-creation, not a translation*
**by HagenGao · free to read and share**

---

Why do we run harder every year, and never seem to get ahead?

The house you can't afford. The kid you'd love to have but can't make the math work for. The retirement that keeps moving further away. The crisis that can empty your savings overnight. These pains look unrelated. This book argues they share one root — and that the root sits in a place no one thinks to look: a single assumption, inherited for centuries and never seriously questioned, that **money must always be positive.**

This book starts from that one idea and follows it all the way down. What if an account could go negative — and the whole of society's books still balanced, always, to exactly zero? That single change turns out to pull on everything. Once you allow it, *capital* — money that earns simply by owning — loses the logical ground it stands on. Not banned. Just no longer able to form.

From there, fourteen chapters trace what a society built on this foundation would actually look like: production and work, the environment, public services, population, innovation, trust, daily life, governance, and where all of it sits in the long arc of human institutions. One formula in the whole book, explained in plain words. Everything else is logic, in the plainest possible language, with an everyday example after every abstract idea.

It does not abolish the market — it keeps the market and removes only the capital inside it. It does not try to remake human nature — it assumes you are exactly as self-interested as you already believe, and works *with* that. It is not a UBI scheme, not a cryptocurrency, not central planning, and not Parecon (Chapter 2 answers, precisely, how it differs from each). And it solves, in one stroke, two problems that have split economics for a century: Marx's problem that value can't be *computed*, and Hayek's problem that information can't be *centralized* — by letting a market with no capital *discover* value instead of either side trying to calculate it.

It is a work of theory. It answers *what a different foundation would look like* and *why it would hold.* It deliberately leaves *how we get there* for another book.

## 🔍 Before you trust it: three appendices

This book asks to be tested, not believed — and it comes with its own testing equipment.

- **Appendix I — The Harshest Objections, and My Answers.** The twelve sharpest attacks on this theory, asked in the attacker's own voice, answered one by one — freeloaders, collusion, induced demand, surveillance, majority tyranny, "isn't this just a planned economy," and the hardest ethical boundary in the book. Where an answer has a limit, the limit is stated.
- **Appendix II — How This Theory Could Be Proven Wrong.** A self-destruct switch, installed by the author: seven observable predictions that a real pilot could confirm — or break. If they fail, the theory is wrong, and the book says so in advance. Read this one first if you're skeptical; it's the fastest way to find out what kind of book this is.
- **Appendix III — The Ones Who Touched the Line.** The honest family tree: Gesell and the money that rusts, Switzerland's WIR circle running negative balances for ninety years, LETS and its zero-sum community ledgers, time banking, MMT's sectoral balances. What each forerunner touched, where each one stopped — and why "isn't this just LETS at scale?" gets a nod first, then a no.
