Benyuan Rewriting the Foundation of the Economy

by HagenGao ｜ English Edition · free to read and share
An account may sink below zero — but a life returns to zero in the end.

Why do we run harder every year, and never seem to get ahead?

The house you can't afford. The kid you'd love to have but can't make the math work for. The retirement that keeps slipping away. The crisis that can empty your savings overnight. These pains look unrelated. This book argues they share one root.

For centuries, one assumption has been inherited by everyone and questioned by no one: that money must always be positive. This book starts from that single idea and follows it down — into a system where an account can go negative, yet the whole of society's books always balance to exactly zero. From there, across fourteen chapters and three appendices — production, work, the environment, public services, population, innovation, trust, daily life, governance, and the long arc of civilization — it traces what a world on that foundation would look like. One formula in the whole book, explained in plain words. Everything else is logic, in the plainest possible language, written for anyone.

🔍 Skeptical? Good. Start here.

This book asks to be tested, not believed — and it ships with its own testing equipment. Appendix II is a self-destruct switch the author installed by hand: seven observable predictions a real pilot could confirm — or break. If they fail, the theory is wrong, and the book says so in advance. Appendix I answers the twelve harshest objections in the attacker's own voice; Appendix III lays out the honest family tree — Gesell, the Swiss WIR circle, LETS, time banking — what each forerunner touched, and where each one stopped.

And for the technically inclined: the book's most load-bearing claims are written out as formal propositions — proofs where proofs exist, labeled assumptions where they don't — in Benyuan Formal Outline: Eight Propositions.
